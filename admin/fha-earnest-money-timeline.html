When the seller
keeps the earnest money

A buyer using FHA financing with a down payment assistance grant. Two things can sink the deal that the seller cannot contract around — a third if the property is part of an HOA or other common interest community. Here is where the deposit actually sits at each stage.

Day0

Offer accepted, deposit made

The brokerage holds the money in trust. Nobody releases it on their own say-so. It moves only three ways: both parties sign a cancellation, a court orders it, or one party completes a statutory cancellation.

Held in trust no claim exists yet
Days0–5

Inspection window

The buyer can walk for almost any reason and take the deposit with them. This window is pure option value for the buyer. Negotiate it short.

Refunded to buyer buyer's discretion
Days5–21

Appraisal, financing, grant approval

Three ways out, all of them refundable: the FHA appraisal comes in low, the loan is denied, or the grant does not fund. A grant that falls through is an unfulfilled financing condition, not cold feet.

Refunded to buyer if notice is timely

Runs the length of the deal

The FHA amendatory clause

If the property appraises below contract price, the buyer may cancel and take back every dollar. This cannot be waived. Not by an appraisal gap clause, not by a waiver in the offer, not by a seller who insists. The only real commitment is a written agreement signed after the appraisal comes in, in which the buyer agrees to bring the difference in cash.

Runs the length of the deal Condo / HOA / CIC only

The CIC resale disclosure right

If the property is part of a homeowners association, condominium, or other common interest community, the seller must furnish a resale disclosure certificate before closing. Once the buyer receives it, they get 10 days to cancel for any reason and recover the full deposit — even if every other contingency deadline has already passed. If the certificate is never furnished, this right does not expire on its own. Confirm the exact trigger and window with counsel for the specific association.

Day21+

Contingencies expire

The buyer is now committed. From here forward, only cold feet puts their deposit at risk. This is the first moment the seller has anything to claim.

ClosingDay

Buyer fails to close

A claim is not the same as money in hand. The seller now has to do something about it. Doing nothing means the deposit sits in trust indefinitely.

Seller has a claim seller must act
PathA

Mutual written cancellation

Both parties sign, the money goes where the document says. Fastest, cheapest, no attorney, no service of process. It requires the buyer's signature, which is why most sellers end up negotiating a split.

Seller has a claim buyer must cooperate
PathB

Statutory cancellation notice

Served like a district court summons, on the buyer and on whoever holds the deposit. An attorney and a process server. The clock starts the day it lands.

Seller has a claim 15-day clock begins

Minn. Stat. § 559.217, subd. 7  /  day 15

Cancellation completes. The earnest money is distributed to and becomes the sole property of the party who completed the cancellation, regardless of anything the purchase agreement says.

Unless the buyer, within those 15 days, cures the default and closes → deal proceeds.
Or secures a court order suspending the cancellation → litigation.
Or serves a cancellation notice of their own → see below.

Any day1–15

The buyer cross-cancels

A single counter-notice from the buyer cancels the purchase agreement immediately and sends the deposit to a judge. The seller's clean win becomes a lawsuit. The prevailing party may recover filing fees, attorney fees, and service costs, capped at $3,000.

Decided by a court or arbitration, if agreed

Six times the seller cannot keep it

No matter what the purchase agreement says, and no matter how badly the seller wants to.

  1. 01

    The appraisal comes in low

    The FHA amendatory clause is mandatory and unwaivable. Full refund.

  2. 02

    The loan is denied

    A buyer who applied in good faith and could not obtain FHA financing keeps their deposit.

  3. 03

    The grant does not fund

    An unfulfilled financing condition. Fold grant approval under a single financing deadline rather than leaving it open-ended.

  4. 04

    Any contingency is timely exercised

    Inspection, financing, appraisal, sale of the buyer's home. The deadline is what matters, not the reason.

  5. 05

    The seller cannot deliver

    Unmarketable title, seller default, or a material disclosure failure under Minn. Stat. § 513.55.

  6. 06

    The HOA/CIC disclosure just arrived

    If the property is in a common interest community, receipt of the resale disclosure certificate opens a fresh 10-day cancellation window under Minn. Stat. § 515B.4-108 — regardless of what other deadlines have already passed.

So what actually protects the seller

The deposit is a deterrent, not a remedy. The protection is structural.

  1. →

    Larger deposit, partly released early

    A defined portion goes to the seller as non-refundable consideration once the inspection contingency expires, credited at closing. Money in hand beats money to argue over.

  2. →

    Short deadlines

    Inspection at five days. Financing and appraisal at twenty-one. Every extra day is free optionality for the buyer.

  3. →

    Underwritten pre-approval, not pre-qualification

    Call the loan officer. Confirm the DPA program has been run and reserved, and that the lender pairs it with FHA.

  4. →

    A backup offer

    The most reliable protection against a buyer walking is having the next one ready.
